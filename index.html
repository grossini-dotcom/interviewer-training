Module 1

The hiring process & your role

Every role runs through the same shape. Knowing where you fit stops candidates from stalling in the pipeline.

Warm-up

Have a go before reading on — guessing is fine, and these don't count towards your score. Attempting first makes the rest stick better.

1You've just finished interviewing a candidate. Where do you think your feedback should go?
The tracking system. It's the single source of truth, it's what candidates can request under GDPR, and it keeps everyone's feedback in one comparable place. Email and Slack scatter it and aren't safe for candidate data. You'll see why below.
2The recruiter is helpful and knows the process well. Whose job is it to actually drive a role forward — comparing feedback, making calls, being ready to offer?
You do. The Talent team supports you, but they aren't the technical experts and can't judge the role for you. Passivity is where candidates stall — the section below spells out what "owning it" means in practice.
◷

How a role kicks off

Before anyone interviews, the Hiring Manager (with the recruiter's support) prepares the scorecard, role specification, any test, the evaluation criteria and the structured questions. A Technical Reviewer may own the test and technical questions instead. The Hiring Manager and recruiter then agree the role details, seniority, salary range and sourcing strategy, and hold a kick-off with all interviewers to finalise the process and questions. Only then does the recruiter open the role in the applicant tracking system.

→

Once the role is live

  • All interview feedback goes into the applicant tracking system — nowhere else.
  • Discuss candidates only in the dedicated role channel; keep sensitive topics (salary, right to work) in the confidential channel.
  • Review tests fast — at most within 24 hours of receiving them.
  • For every rejection from screening onwards, give the recruitment team constructive feedback.
  • Review divergent feedback quickly and make clear decisions to progress or offer.
◆

You own the requirements. The Talent team supports you, but they aren't the technical experts. Drive the process forward — make decisions, compare feedback, be ready to offer. Don't be passive, and advertise the role through your own network too.

Knowledge check
QWhere must interview feedback be recorded?
Correct: the applicant tracking system. Feedback lives there and nowhere else — it's the single source of truth and it's what candidates can request under GDPR. Slack channels get deleted, and candidate data doesn't belong in email.
Knowledge check
QA candidate submits their take-home test. What's the expectation on reviewing it?
Within 24 hours. Slow reviews are where good candidates go cold and accept other offers. Reviewing promptly keeps the pipeline moving and respects the effort they put in — waiting to batch-compare everyone costs you the people you most want.

Module 2

Running a good interview

An interview is a two-way sell. You're assessing the candidate, but they're also deciding whether they want to join us.

Warm-up

Try these before reading on — guessing is fine, and they don't count towards your score.

1A candidate is clearly struggling with a question and looking anxious. What's the best instinct?
Support them. You're not there to catch anyone out, and a "bad cop" act tells you little except how someone copes with being treated badly. A supported candidate gives you a truer read of their ability — and leaves with a good impression of us either way.
2Which question is likely to give you the most useful evidence?
The second. It's open-ended and asks for a real example, so the candidate has to show you rather than just claim. The first invites a yes; the third is a leading question that hands them the answer. More on open questions and follow-ups below.
♦

We don't need a bad cop. Being interviewed is hard. Support the candidate the way you'd support a colleague — it isn't an exam, and you're not there to catch them out. Happy candidates are far less likely to be upset by anything, even a rejection.

☺

Candidate experience

  • Introduce yourself — say what you do, describe the team and the role.
  • Be respectful and friendly; don't leave them to struggle.
  • Remember we're selling ourselves to them, too.
  • Always leave time for their questions.
?

Interview technique

Four habits that separate a useful interview from a pleasant chat:

Prepare, and set a timing structure. Ask the Talent team for support or training. Going in with a plan and a rough time budget for each section keeps you from running out of road before you've covered what matters.
Ask open-ended questions. Avoid yes/no and unintended leading questions. Base questions on the candidate's real prior experience, and don't guide them toward the "right" answer — unless you're consciously choosing to support them.
Investigate with follow-ups. Explore. Get the details, and the blind alleys they went down. If a first answer doesn't convince you, come at the issue from another angle to check rather than concluding on too little.
Share a narrative, and trust your judgement. Tell the story of the company, the team and where the role fits. Respect your own knowledge, and look for evidence both for and against each interview criterion.
▤

Structured questions

Pick the criteria you're checking, set a topic, and build a small framework of questions inside it. Favour real work examples over hypotheticals, and record evidence for and against the criteria — not just a gut feeling.

Knowledge check
QA candidate gives a vague answer and you're not convinced. What's the best move?
Explore before you conclude. A weak first answer isn't always a weak candidate — subtle follow-ups from a different angle let you check properly. Hinting contaminates the evidence, and giving up too early means you score on too little.
Knowledge check
QAt the end of an interview, what should your score rest on?
Evidence, recorded for and against each criterion. A gut feeling is exactly where bias hides — and it's impossible for the rest of the panel to sense-check. Concrete examples tied to the criteria make your score fair, defensible, and comparable with other interviewers'.

Module 3

The three pillars

We assess every candidate against three cultural pillars: Critical Thinking, Teamwork and Hunger. They're the backbone of the scorecard — the skills you gather evidence for throughout the interview, and what you'll score 1–5 in the next module.

Warm-up

Have a go at these before reading on — it's fine to guess. Attempting first makes the explanations underneath stick better, and these don't count towards your score.

1A candidate solves a problem quickly, but when you ask how they reached it, they say "it just felt right" and can't point to any data or reasoning. Which pillar does this most raise a question about?
Critical Thinking. A fast answer isn't the concern — the absence of checked assumptions, data or root-cause reasoning is. Critical Thinking is about how someone reaches a conclusion, not how quickly. You'd want to probe further before scoring it low, but this is the pillar it speaks to.
2Two candidates both "share ideas well." One does it only when things are calm; the other keeps doing it under pressure and helps teammates do the same. In pillar terms, what separates them?
It's the level, not the pillar. Both are Teamwork. The difference between a 3 and a 4–5 is exactly this: doing it consistently even under pressure (4), and mentoring others to do it too (5). The pillar tells you what you're looking at; the level tells you how strongly it shows up.

Here's what each pillar covers, and what the levels beneath it actually mean.

◈

Critical Thinking

Critical thinking + Work on the problem. Seeking data, checking assumptions, finding root causes, and the consistency, novelty and quality of outcomes.

◈

Teamwork

Share ideas + Love what you do. Sharing feedback, knowledge and ideas, a positive attitude and motivation, and dealing well with pressure.

◈

Hunger

Ownership + Keep it simple. Awareness of goals, ownership, goal-orientation, weighing cost-benefit tradeoffs, and creating high-impact solutions.

What each score looks like

Each pillar has a 1–5 behavioural definition. Two patterns run through all three: the jump from 3 to 4 is about doing it consistently and independently rather than only with guidance, and the jump from 4 to 5 is about lifting others — a 5 doesn't just do it well themselves, they raise the people around them. Keep those in mind as you read.

Critical Thinking · 1 to 5

1
Absent. Doesn't question assumptions, doesn't seek data or other perspectives, acts on their first hypothesis, and doesn't investigate root causes.
2
Low. Inconsistently checks their own assumptions, or only sometimes seeks data and root causes.
3
Average. Examines assumptions, root causes and data — but may require guidance to do so.
4
Good. Consistently examines assumptions, root causes and data; produces high-quality, novel problem-solving or original outcomes; acts independently.
5
Exceptional. Encourages and guides others to improve their own critical thinking, helping them address both immediate symptoms and root causes.

Teamwork · 1 to 5

1
Absent. No sharing of feedback, knowledge or ideas; no motivation, or a negative attitude.
2
Low. Inconsistently shares feedback, knowledge and ideas; occasionally motivated but mainly neutral or negative.
3
Average. Consistently shares feedback, knowledge and ideas but may need guidance to do so; some motivation and a positive attitude to colleagues, but can waver under pressure.
4
Good. Consistently shares high-quality feedback, ideas and knowledge without guidance; high motivation and a positive attitude to their role and colleagues, even under pressure.
5
Exceptional. Shares, mentors and promotes the giving of feedback, sharing of ideas and knowledge, and motivation among colleagues.

Hunger · 1 to 5

1
Absent. Limited awareness of goals, inconsistent ownership; complicated, time-consuming or low-quality solutions.
2
Low. Takes some ownership and shows some goal-orientation; occasionally simplifies solutions, or solutions are low-impact.
3
Average. Takes ownership of their actions, decisions and outcomes and learns from them; goal-oriented, understands cost-benefit analysis, prioritises high-impact solutions — but may need support.
4
Good. Continuously improves; highly goal-oriented; connects their work to business objectives; independently develops high-quality, consistently high-impact solutions.
5
Exceptional. A value champion — mentors or supports others in taking ownership, goal-orientation and understanding business needs and costs, and encourages the development of high-impact solutions.
◆

"This is too hard!" You don't need a perfect read on all five levels in the room. Gather pieces of evidence — from general questions, relevant scenarios and direct questions — and for each pillar decide one of three things: the candidate lacks the skill, it's not clear, or the candidate has the skill. That alone is enough to place a score honestly, and "not clear" is a legitimate answer that tells the team where to dig next.

Building pillar questions

Develop your questions with the recruitment team, and build the pillars into your structured questions rather than bolting them on at the end. Favour prompts that make the candidate describe real behaviour, then follow up for specifics — for example:

  • "Tell me about what you understand by good teamwork." (Teamwork)
  • "What drives you to succeed and improve?" (Hunger)
  • "How do you apply data to your decision-making?" (Critical Thinking)
Knowledge check
QA candidate independently examines assumptions, seeks out data and digs into root causes, with consistently high-quality outcomes. Which pillar and level does this describe?
Critical Thinking, level 4. Examining assumptions, seeking data and finding root causes are the Critical Thinking behaviours; doing it consistently, independently and with high quality is level 4. Level 5 would add actively guiding others to think this way.
Knowledge check
QYou gathered only a little evidence on a candidate's Hunger, and what you saw was mixed. What's the right move?
"Not clear" is the honest call. A 3 claims you assessed it and found it average; a 1 claims you assessed it and found it absent. Neither is true when the evidence is thin. Naming it "not clear" tells the team exactly where the next interviewer should focus — which is more useful than a falsely confident number.

Module 4

Scoring & feedback

We grade each skill from 1 to 5. Consistent scoring is what lets us compare candidates fairly and calibrate over time.

Warm-up

Give these a go before reading on — guessing is fine, and they don't count towards your score.

1You finished the interview 20 minutes ago but haven't written anything down yet. Your afternoon is packed. What's the priority?
Fast yes/no first. The recruiter can act on a clear overall lean straight away, which keeps the candidate moving; the written detail can follow within 24 hours. Memory fades fast, so don't leave it to the meeting — jot evidence while it's fresh.
2You're writing a scorecard for a candidate you're rejecting. What tone should it take?
Professional and specific. Candidates can request their scorecards under GDPR, and they can surface in a discrimination claim — so "internal" is never really private. Vagueness is its own risk: unexplained decisions look arbitrary. Say what you decided and why, grounded in the criteria.

The 1–5 scale

1
AbsentAbsent or very weak.
2
LowInconsistent, low-quality outcomes, or needs a lot of guidance.
3
AverageConsistent, average outcomes; may need guidance in more complex situations.
4
GoodHigh-quality, creative or innovative outcomes; works independently.
5
ExceptionalWould raise the level of the team; a value champion who helps others improve.

How it looks in Greenhouse

In Greenhouse, each skill on the scorecard has a row of five icons running left to right, lowest to highest. The one you click is your score for that skill:

Critical thinking 3 · Average
Teamwork 4 · Good
Hunger left blank — no evidence

Left to right: ✕ = 1 (absent), thumbs-down = 2 (low), neutral = 3 (average), thumbs-up = 4 (good), star = 5 (exceptional). A skill you couldn't assess stays unselected.

Once you've rated each skill, Greenhouse asks for an overall judgement on the candidate (a yes/no leaning) and a free-text summary for the team. When you're finished, click Submit Scorecard — feedback that isn't submitted can't be seen by the rest of the panel.

▲

No evidence? Leave it blank. Don't default to a 3 — a 3 is read as "I checked this skill and it's average." Blank means "I didn't gather enough evidence to judge," which is honest and useful. These are very different signals.

◷

Speed matters. We need an overall yes/no as soon as possible so the process can keep moving. If you can't complete your scorecard within an hour or two, flag it in the dedicated channel — a fast yes/no lets the recruiter act while you finish the written detail. Complete all feedback within 24 hours.

Writing it up

  • Keep the tone professional — candidates can request their scorecards under GDPR.
  • Be specific and evidence-based; say why you reached a decision.
  • It's fine to flag areas of concern or "needs further assessment" if you're unsure.
  • When rejecting, pick one main reason from the agreed criteria — multiple reasons are fine, but name the key one.
  • Remember to actually submit the scorecard when you're done.
Knowledge check
QYou didn't gather enough evidence to judge a candidate's data skills. What do you enter?
Blank. A 3 says you assessed the skill and found it average; a 1 says you assessed it and found it absent. Neither is true if you simply didn't get evidence. Blank keeps your scorecard honest and tells the team this skill still needs checking.
Knowledge check
QYou've filled in every rating and written your summary. You get pulled into a meeting. What still needs to happen?
Submit the scorecard. Filled-in but unsubmitted feedback is invisible to the rest of the panel — a surprisingly common way for a decision to stall. And don't route it via email; the scorecard system is the only place candidate feedback should live.

Module 5

Protected characteristics

These are traits the law protects from discrimination. You must not ask about them, record them, or let them enter a hiring decision — directly or indirectly.

Warm-up

Have a go before reading on — guessing is fine, and these don't count towards your score.

1To break the ice, you're tempted to ask a candidate "So, do you have kids?" Is that okay in an interview?
No. However warmly it's meant, it asks about family and pregnancy — protected characteristics — and asking everyone doesn't make it safe. Save the rapport for topics that can't disadvantage anyone. The list below shows what's protected.
2A job spec asks for "10+ years' experience," though the role doesn't truly need that much. Could that be a problem?
Yes — this is indirect discrimination. A neutral-looking rule that isn't genuinely necessary can disadvantage a protected group (here, by age). If a requirement isn't essential to the job, it shouldn't gate candidates. You'll see direct vs. indirect explained below.
⊘

Don't ask about, or record, any of these

  • Race, nationality or ethnic origin
  • Sex
  • Pregnancy, family or children
  • Sexual orientation
  • Religion, belief or political opinion
  • Gender reassignment
  • Disability (physical or mental, incl. HIV status)
  • Age
  • Marriage & civil partnership status
  • Trade union membership

Grounded in the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights (Art. 21), the UK Equality Act 2010, and equivalent national law. Some countries protect more — France, for example, recognises 25 characteristics.

Direct vs. indirect discrimination

Direct discrimination. Treating someone worse because of a protected characteristic — for example, not hiring someone because of their age. A direct question would be "How old are you?"
Indirect discrimination. A requirement that looks neutral but unfairly disadvantages a protected group. Demanding a degree the role doesn't actually need can disadvantage certain candidates. Best practice: avoid "years of experience" and non-essential requirements on the spec.
Questions that could be read that way. Even indirect probes count. "Are you planning on having a family?" discriminates on sex and pregnancy. Before asking anything, pause: could this be interpreted as asking about a protected characteristic, or disadvantage people who have one?
✓

What you can ask about

  • Role competency — anything directly related to doing the job, ideally the core criteria set by the Hiring Manager.
  • Professional abilities — communication, motivation, initiative, management skills.
  • Language skills — the ability to use languages the role genuinely needs. You can require fluency and reject if it's insufficient.
◆

Keep it simple. The recruitment team handles right to work, location, salary and reasonable adjustments. Focus on professional requirements. If a candidate volunteers protected information in casual chat, that's fine socially — but don't record it and don't let it influence your decision. Keep social conversation clearly separate from the interview.

When in doubt, don't ask

Rules differ by country, and some questions are legal in one place and not another. Don't rely on legality as your test — we hold to a higher, consistent standard everywhere: if a question is personal rather than about the job, leave it out, whether or not it's technically allowed. A few common examples to steer clear of:

  • Salary — current, previous, or expectations. Leave the whole topic to the recruitment team; you don't need to raise it at all.
  • Whether someone uses drugs, drinks, or their health and lifestyle.
  • Whether they're a "native" speaker — you can require the fluency the role genuinely needs, but that's about the role, not their background.

The safe habit is simple: if you're unsure whether a question is okay, it isn't one you need to ask — so don't. The recruitment team can handle anything that genuinely needs answering.

Knowledge check
QMid-interview, a candidate mentions in passing that they've just come back from maternity leave. What do you do with that?
Let it pass, don't record it. A candidate volunteering something protected is fine socially — but the moment it enters your notes or your judgement, it's discrimination. Keep the casual chat and the assessment cleanly separate.
Knowledge check
QThe role genuinely requires strong written English. Which is the fair way to assess that?
Assess the skill the role needs. Requiring genuine fluency is fine — it's about the job. "Native speaker" and "where did you grow up" are about background and nationality, which are protected and don't actually measure whether someone can do the work.

Module 6

Safe or not safe?

This whole module is practice: six real questions, and for each you decide whether it's safe to ask a candidate before revealing why. It's the flip side of what you just read — put the "when in doubt, don't ask" instinct to work. Don't worry about getting them all right first time; the explanations are where the learning is. Answer all six to continue.

1"Are you married?"
Not safe. This is a direct question about marital status — a protected characteristic.
2"What do you enjoy doing outside of work?"
Best avoided. Not obviously discriminatory, but it's overly broad and invites protected information. If a hobby is genuinely relevant to the role, ask about that skill directly and consistently instead.
3"Where are you from?"
Not safe. It asks about nationality — a protected characteristic. Even answered as a current location, it can imply discrimination if that place is associated with an ethnicity. Don't ask it in either sense.
4"How do you feel about managing a team of men?"
Not safe. It implies a woman might find managing men harder — discriminating on sex.
5"When did you graduate?"
Not safe. A graduation date is a way of asking someone's age.
6"Can you write in English?"
Safe — if the role needs it. If writing in English is a genuine requirement of the job, you can ask this.

Module 7

Unconscious bias

We all have it — that's normal, and it's not a moral failing. The goal isn't to pretend you're free of bias; it's to notice it and deliberately push the other way, gathering evidence that challenges your first impression rather than confirms it.

Warm-up

Try these first — guessing is fine, and they don't count towards your score. They'll make the explanations below land better.

1Ten minutes into an interview you think "yes, this is our person." For the rest of the call you find yourself mostly noticing the things that prove you right. What's happening?
This is confirmation bias. The early verdict quietly changes how you listen — supporting evidence gets weight, contradicting evidence slides past. The danger isn't having a first impression; it's letting it filter everything that follows. You'll see the full definition below.
2A candidate worked at a company you admire. You catch yourself assuming they'll be strong across every skill, including ones you haven't tested yet. What should you do?
This is the halo effect — and the fix isn't to overcorrect. One strong signal shouldn't inflate the others, but deliberately marking them down would just be a different distortion. The move is to assess each skill on its own evidence. More on this below.

Here are the three that show up most in interviews. Read them with your warm-up answers in mind.

Affinity bias

An unconscious preference for people who are similar to you — the same background, interests, career path, sense of humour, or way of speaking. You simply click with them more, the conversation flows, and that ease gets misread as evidence of competence. It's one of the most common biases in hiring precisely because it feels like nothing more than "good rapport."

What it looks like: you leave the interview saying "I really liked them" but struggle to point to specific, role-relevant evidence behind the warmth. The counter-move: when you notice that easy rapport, treat it as a flag to work harder, not a reason to relax — deliberately probe for concrete examples so your score rests on what they can do, not on how comfortable the conversation felt.

Confirmation bias

Forming an early impression and then, often without realising, seeking out information that confirms it while dismissing anything that contradicts it. Once you've decided someone is strong, an ambiguous answer gets read generously; once you've decided someone is weak, the same answer gets read harshly. The impression shapes the evidence instead of the evidence shaping the impression.

What it looks like: your questions in the second half of the interview start leaning towards the verdict you already reached. The counter-move: hold your judgement open as long as you can, and actively look for evidence against your first read — ask yourself "what would change my mind?" and then genuinely test it.

Halo & horns effect

When one strong impression bleeds into everything else. The halo: a single positive — a prestigious employer, a confident manner, a shared alma mater — makes you assume the person is excellent across the board, including areas you haven't actually assessed. The horns is the same mechanism in reverse: one negative, like a nervous start or a single weak answer, colours your whole view and drags down skills that might genuinely be strong.

What it looks like: a candidate's rating on one pillar mysteriously matches their rating on all the others, with little independent evidence for each. The counter-move: score each pillar separately and make yourself cite evidence for each one on its own. If you can't find distinct evidence for a skill, that's a signal to leave it "not clear" — not to borrow confidence from an unrelated strength or weakness.

♦

The counter-move is the same shape every time. Whenever you notice a strong pull — positive or negative — treat it as a prompt to go looking for concrete, specific evidence rather than acting on the feeling. Bias thrives on vague impressions; it struggles against "show me a real example." Let the evidence decide.

Scenario check
QA candidate went to your university and you immediately warm to them. Which bias is this, and what should you do?
Affinity bias. The shared background is creating the warmth, not the candidate's actual ability. Notice it, then push the other way — ask for specific evidence and examples so your score rests on what they can do, not where they've been.
Scenario check
QA candidate is visibly nervous and stumbles through the first question. You notice yourself starting to feel sceptical about everything they say afterwards. What's the risk, and the fix?
Horns effect. One weak moment — especially nerves, which aren't a pillar — is colouring your read of everything after it. The fix is to notice the drag and deliberately assess each subsequent answer freshly, giving the candidate the room to recover that you'd want yourself. Supporting a nervous candidate is also just good candidate experience.

Module 8

Data & GDPR

Candidate data is personal data, and the law is strict about it. The rules are simple to follow once you know them.

Warm-up

Try these before reading on — guessing is fine, and they don't count towards your score.

1You want to jot a few thoughts during an interview. Where should those notes end up afterwards?
Into the tracking system, then gone. Scratch notes — digital or paper — are candidate personal data. Move the substance into the scorecard and destroy the original; loose notes lying around are exactly what GDPR is strict about.
2Roughly how big can a GDPR fine be? (A sense of scale, not a trick.)
Up to €20 million or 4% of global turnover, whichever is greater. The point isn't to frighten you — it's why these small habits matter. Following the simple rules below is what keeps both the candidate and the company safe.
▤

The rules for interviewers

  • Interview notes — keep them professional, role-related and evidence-based. Candidates can request them under GDPR, or they can surface as evidence in a discrimination case.
  • Email — no candidate data in emails; let the recruitment team manage communications.
  • Slack — discuss candidates only in the dedicated role channels, which are deleted after use.
  • Downloads — don't download or save candidate data.
  • Notes — any digital or physical notes must be transferred into the tracking system, then deleted or destroyed.

A note on how recruiters differ

Recruiters operate under a few adjusted rules — for example, they may hold candidate data in email under a one-year deletion policy, and they manage the auto-deletion of downloaded files. As an interviewer you don't need these exceptions; the tighter rules above are yours. If you're ever unsure whether something's allowed, ask the recruitment team rather than guessing.

▲

Why this genuinely matters. Breaching GDPR — for instance, downloading a candidate's CV to a device and keeping it — can expose the company to a maximum fine of €20 million or 4% of annual global turnover, whichever is greater. Following these rules protects candidates and protects the company. Please look after both.

Knowledge check
QA candidate emails you their CV directly. What should you do?
Don't download or save it. Candidate data belongs in the tracking system, managed by recruitment — not on your device or spread across emails. Saving CVs locally is exactly the kind of breach that carries those fines.
Knowledge check
QYou want a colleague's quick take on a candidate. Where should that conversation happen?
The dedicated role channel. Those channels are set up for candidate discussion and deleted after use, which keeps data contained. Scattering it into DMs and email is how candidate information ends up in places no one can properly clean up later.
